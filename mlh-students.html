Introduction
The Technion-MLH Challenge, denoted ‘Challenge’ in the following, aims to stimulate rapid progress on an unsolved question of practical clinical significance and that may benefit improving healthcare outcomes for the citizen of Israel and globally. For that purpose, data will be provided to the participants at the beginning of the course together with the medical question of interest. The dataset consists of a collection of variables and/or physiological signals that need to be analyzed toward a defined goal. The participants are provided with a subset of the data, the “training set” that will be used by each team to develop their algorithm and a “test set” that will not be provided to the team and which will be used to analyze the participants’ algorithm performance. The data and question we ask are novel and you will be the first to research it! The Horef 2019 Challenge in partnership with the Rambam Children’s hospital addresses the following research question: For children admitted with bronchiolitis at the Rambam, predict whether the length of stay will be prolonged (superior to 4.5 days) by a prior exposure to an increased air pollution level? In the context of this research “length of stay” is used as a proxy for the severity of bronchiolitis and so the underlying question we ask is whether the air pollution level is an independent variable increasing the severity of bronchiolitis cases that were admitted at the Rambam. Retrospective data covering the past 20 years will be used.

Medical background
Viral bronchiolitis is the most common reason for hospitalizing young infants in the winter season1. The diagnosis of bronchiolitis is a clinical one: difficulty breathing, coryza, cough, wheeze and crepitations on chest auscultation. The disease is associated with viral infection. Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) accounts for 50–80%2. It has a seasonal pattern, with the highest incidence in the winter months3. Over the last several decades, a growing body of evidence has linked short-term and long-term exposures to ambient air pollution with respiratory and cardiovascular diseases and mortality. Certain particulate and gaseous pollutants - especially particulate matter, which is subdivided depending on particle size as either less than 2.5 mm - PM2.5, or less than 10 mm -PM10 -, have a clinically relevant effect on hospital admissions for bronchiolitis in children below age two years old4. Yet, there is paucity of data on the impact of air pollution on severity of disease in bronchiolitis.

Variables
Data on air pollution (PM2.5, PM10 and Nox), humidity and temperature were collected from monitoring stations available from the ministry environmental protection website (https://svivaaqm.net) from 2000 onwards. Information on patients admitted and diagnosed with bronchiolitis were extracted from the Rambam medical record system from year 2000 onwards. Patients zip code were mapped to the closest monitoring station location with available data and up to a 30km radius around the patient location. The following variables are provided:
Target variable:

    • Length of stay: this is the time patients stayed under observation at the Rambam. A length of stay that is superior to 4.5 days will be considered as prolonged and used as a proxy for the severity of bronchiolitis. This variable is provided both as a continuous variable of the total number of days of stay and as a binary variable with 0: stay is less than 4.5 days and 1: stay is superior or equal to 4.5 days. The Challenge scoring system will assess your predictions against the binary variable but we provide the continuous variable in case you want to explore new avenues!
      Patients variables:
      • Gender: male/female (binary).
          Comorbidities: History of prematurity, respiratory disease, cardiac disease, metabolic disease genetic disease, developmental delay, neurological, respiratory- are provided as binary variables.
            Age at diagnosis: continuous variable (double).
              Ethnicity: as a categorical variable, Jewish: 1, Muslim Arab: 2, Christian Arab: 3, Druze: 4.
                Birth delivery week: continuous variable (integer).
                  Birth-weight (g): continuous variable (double).
                  The following variables are provided for the 30 days prior to admission to the Rambam hospital:
                  • PM2.5: fine particles (or particulate matter, PM) with a diameter of 2.5µm or less.
                      PM10: inhalable coarse particles which are coarse particles with a diameter below 10µm.
                        Nox: Nitrogen dioxide is an irritant gas, which at high concentrations causes inflammation of the airways.
                          Temperature: in degree Celsius.
                            Relative humidity: the ratio of the partial pressure of water vapor to the equilibrium vapor pressure of water at a given temperature.
