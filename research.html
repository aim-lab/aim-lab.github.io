The Artificial Intelligence in Medicine Laboratory (AIMLab.) researches innovative pattern recognition algorithms to exploit the information encrypted within large datasets of physiological time series. AIMLab. leverages these new data-driven algorithms toward the creation of novel intelligent remote patient monitoring systems.

With billions of mobile devices worldwide, the low cost of mobile-connected medical sensors, and the widespread availability and use of medical imaging apparatuses, recording and transmitting medical data has become easier than ever. However, this ‘wealth’ of medical data has had limited success in providing novel actionable clinical information to improve patient care within and outside the traditional clinical environment. This is due to the lack of robust intelligent algorithms that can exploit the information encrypted within these ‘big databases’ of physiological time-series and medical images and take individual variability into account. Exploiting these datasets necessitates an in-depth understanding of the physiology underlying the patterns present in the medical time series and images, and use of advanced digital signal processing and machine learning (ML) tools (e.g., deep learning) to recognize and extract patterns characteristic of health function in order to translate these patterns into clinically actionable information.


Within the context of this challenge, the Technion AIMLab. focuses on the following topics of medical AI:

● Classification tasks using supervised learning and semi-supervised learning for diagnosis using large datasets and with a focus on the clinical generalization capabilities of the models to independent test databases.
● Phenotyping using unsupervised learning to identify categories of patients which are distinguished from others through a single or combination of disease features in relation to clinically meaningful attributes such as response to therapy or the development of a clinical endpoint.
● Interpretability of classical ML models and deep learning models, causal inference to understand the causal relationship between clinical features and a clinical endpoint and mathematical modelling to support understanding the etiology of diseases.
● Medical ethics in the age of ML and big data: patient privacy and confidentiality, data fairness, transparency, and liability and, ultimately, how ML is to be integrated in clinical practice.

COVID-19

AI during pandemics

Severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2, abbreviated SARS-CoV-2 for the virus or COVID-19 for the syndrome, pneumonia is a newly recognized illness that initially spread throughout Wuhan (Hubei province), from there, to other provinces in China and then across the globe. Millions of individuals have been contaminated and hundred of thousands have died because of it. The clinical spectrum of SARS-CoV-2 pneumonia ranges from mild to critically ill cases.

COVID-19 is not the first pandemic, and it will not be the last. There is a need for creating a sustainable research resource that enables transferring and using de-identified medical data (e.g. clinical variables, radiology images, physiological time series) from Israeli hospitals in a rapid, efficient and secured manner. This is the basis to enable a fast response to the current and next disease X epidemic. SARS-CoV-2-associated data extracted from the local population will be of considerable value for identifying population specific risk factors for developing the diseases and becoming critically ill as well as for monitoring the long-term consequences of recovered patients. Specifically, this research aims to:

● Create “COV19” (https://cov19-resource.com/), a dynamic COVID-19 medical data record resource of de-identified patient’s structured and unstructured data.
● Research clinical biomarkers characteristics of COVID-19 at the time of the first hospital presentation and during the course of hospitalization.
● Study the long-term effect of COVID-19 for individuals that recovered from the condition.
● Review the effect of the pandemic on remote health monitoring usage and adoption.



From sleep medicine to medicine during sleep

A data-driven paradigm change

To date, the field of sleep medicine has focused on the diagnosis of sleep disorders as defined in the International Classification of Sleep Disorders (ICSD-3). Among those, due to its high prevalence, obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is often the main focus, sometimes the sole interest of clinically working sleep centers. Yet, although we spend, on average, one third of our time sleeping, less research has been invested in characterizing how non-sleep-specific diseases manifest during sleep. These ‘secondary sleep disorders’ are mentioned specifically in the ICSD-3. Indeed, diseases regulation and expression during sleep might differ significantly than during wakefulness.

We hypothesize that some conditions may be better expressed during sleep thus providing an added prognosis value. Therefore, sleep may be a particularly suitable time for performing diagnosis and monitoring of many conditions, such as some cardiovascular and respiratory diseases. Indeed, if disease-specific physiological patterns manifesting during sleep are identified, these may be leveraged to perform diagnosis and monitoring of medical conditions while sleeping. In addition, during sleep individuals remains more still than during wakefulness. This enables hours of data acquirement with reduced noise, overall providing longer recordings. The long and quality recordings may then be automatically analyzed by artificial intelligence (AI) algorithms to identify patterns of health and disease. This means, sleep can be regarded as a ‘testbed’ for physiological functions. This paradigm, recently coined ‘medicine during sleep’ (Behar 2020), is the topic of this research axis.

We focus our research on this new paradigm in how diagnosis and monitoring of human health may be performed during sleep. It aims to encourage novel and creative ways to assess human health during sleep for any medical condition.



Atrial fibrillation diagnosis and phenotyping

Data-driven continuous remote monitoring

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common arrhythmia, with a prevalence of 0.95% among the adult population. It is associated with quivering or irregular heartbeat, that can lead to blood clots, stroke, heart failure and other heart-related complications. The currently accepted convention for AF diagnosis is the presence of an episode lasting at least 30 seconds. It has been shown that further characterization of the condition over longer intervals, lasting from hours to days, may improve phenotyping of the diseases, and some recent research has started investigating the regularity and length of AF events across individuals. In addition, because a large proportion of AF individuals present paroxysmal AF this means that long recordings must be performed to accurately diagnose them. This motivates performing long-term continuous electrophysiological recording for the purpose of robust AF diagnosis. On the electrocardiogram (ECG) signal, AF is characterized by an irregular heart rate and by the absence of p-wave. Today, ECG may be recorded using a single portable channel such as the Zio patch (iRhythm, San Francisco). We aim to develop advanced ML approaches to accurately diagnose AF from hours long recordings of a single channel ECG time series and better phenotype patients using unsupervised learning.

This research is expected to contribute novel algorithms drawing from the most recent advances in deep learning and applied to AF personalized diagnosis and treatment. The outcome may result in a set of algorithms that can translate to the clinical practice and significantly decrease morbidity and mortality associated with AF. Given the high prevalence of AF (0.95% among the adult population), the impact of such new technology may be significant.



Fetal electrocardiography

Source separation for remote fetal monitoring

The US records 26,000 fetal death each year. Half of all stillbirths are caused by pregnancy disorders and conditions affecting the placenta. The aim of antenatal fetal surveillance is to identify fetuses at risk of intrauterine hypoxia and academia. Timely, appropriate intervention will avoid fetal neurological damage or death. Currently, women with at risk pregnancy must go to the hospital daily for assessing the fetal wellbeing. This is stressful for the women and sometime impossible because of the lack of local specialized center.

The aim of this research is to elaborate a portable fetal monitor based on non-invasive fetal electrocardiography (Holter NI-FECG). Such a monitor can be used for regular remote assessment of fetal health in pregnancies at risk or to follow-up on treatments. The elaboration of a NI-FECG Holter device will offer new opportunities for fetal diagnosis and remote monitoring of problematic pregnancies because of its low-cost, non-invasiveness, portability and minimal set-up requirements. Specifically, this research aims to:

● Source separation algorithms for extracting the fetal electrocardiogram including deep neural networks and blind source separation algorithms.
● Modelling of the fetal and maternal heart activities and how factors such as tissues conduction and fetal position affect the quality and amplitude of the surface fetal electrocardiogram.
